What is English?

English is one of the most widely spoken languages in the world, with over 1.5 billion speakers. It's used in business, education, science, travel, and the internet. Learning English opens doors to countless opportunities.

üí° Why Learn English?

Over 50% of all internet content is in English. It's the official language of aviation, science publications, and international business. It's also the language of many great books, films, and music.

English belongs to the Germanic language family and uses the Latin alphabet (26 letters). It has borrowed words from French, Latin, Greek, and many other languages ‚Äî which is why it has such a rich vocabulary.

The Alphabet & Sounds

The English alphabet has 26 letters ‚Äî 5 vowels and 21 consonants. Knowing how each letter sounds is the first step to reading and speaking English.

Vowels

The 5 vowels are: A, E, I, O, U. Every English word has at least one vowel sound. Vowels can make different sounds depending on the word.

üìñ Vowel Examples
A ‚Üí apple, cat, age    E ‚Üí egg, bed, me    I ‚Üí ink, big, night    O ‚Üí open, dog    U ‚Üí up, cup, use

Consonants

The 21 consonants are all the other letters. Some consonants make surprising sounds in certain words. For example: C can sound like "k" (cat) or "s" (city).

üîë Remember

In English, pronunciation doesn't always match spelling! The best way to improve is to listen and practice speaking out loud every day.

Nouns & Pronouns

Nouns

A noun is a word for a person, place, thing, or idea.

TypeDefinitionExamples

PlaceA locationschool, city, India, park
ThingAn objectbook, phone, computer, car
IdeaAn abstract conceptlove, freedom, knowledge

Singular & Plural Nouns

Most nouns become plural (more than one) by adding -s or -es.

Verbs & Tenses

A verb is an action or state word ‚Äî it tells you what someone does or is. Every sentence in English must have a verb.

üìñ Common Verbs
be, have, do, say, go, get, make, know, think, take, come, see, want, look, use, find, give, tell, work, feel

The 3 Main Tenses

TenseWhen?StructureExample
PastAlready happenedSubject + verb-edShe walked to school.
PresentHappening now / always trueSubject + verbHe walks to school.
FutureWill happenSubject + will + verbThey will walk to school.

Present Continuous (Right Now)

Use this tense to describe something happening at this exact moment. Structure: Subject + is/are + verb-ing

‚úì Examples
I am learning English.
She is reading a book.
They are playing football.
‚úó Common Mistakes
I am learn English.
She is read a book.
They are play football.
‚ö†Ô∏è Irregular Verbs

Some verbs don't follow the -ed rule: go ‚Üí went, have ‚Üí had, see ‚Üí saw, come ‚Üí came, know ‚Üí knew. You'll need to memorise these!

Adjectives & Adverbs

Adjectives

An adjective describes a noun ‚Äî it tells you more about a person, place, or thing. In English, adjectives come before the noun they describe.

üìñ Examples
a big house    a beautiful day    a fast car    a friendly teacher    a difficult question

Comparing with Adjectives

TypeRuleExample
Normaladjectivetall, fast, good
Comparative (2 things)adjective + -er / more + adjtaller, faster, better
Superlative (3+ things)the + adjective + -est / mostthe tallest, the most beautiful

Adverbs

An adverb describes a verb ‚Äî it tells you how, when, or where something happens. Most adverbs are made by adding -ly to an adjective.

üìñ Adjective ‚Üí Adverb
slow ‚Üí slowly    quick ‚Üí quickly    careful ‚Üí carefully    quiet ‚Üí quietly    happy ‚Üí happily

Building Sentences

A basic English sentence follows the SVO pattern: Subject ‚Üí Verb ‚Üí Object.

üìñ SVO Structure
Kishor (Subject) + reads (Verb) + books (Object)

The cat + eats + fish.
She + loves + music.
They + built + a website.

Questions

To turn a statement into a question, swap the subject and auxiliary verb:

StatementQuestion
She is a teacher.Is she a teacher?
He can swim.Can he swim?
They have a car.Do they have a car?
You like coffee.Do you like coffee?

Negatives

Add not (or don't / doesn't / didn't) to make a sentence negative:

‚úì Negative
I do not like rain.
She doesn't eat meat.
They didn't come.
‚úó Incorrect
I not like rain.
She not eat meat.
They not came.
